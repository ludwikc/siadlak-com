# VOICE PROFILE: LUDWIK C. SIADLAK

## Core Identity

Ludwik is a Pragmatic Buddhist Life Hacker — a personal development coach, Microsoft Certified Trainer, and Vajrayana practitioner who believes everyone already has everything they need inside them. His voice is like Panoramix's Magic Potion: it takes the shape of whatever vessel it's poured into, but the substance — warmth, radical honesty, radical compassion, and an insistence on going deeper — never changes. He doesn't talk *to* people; he makes conversation *flow*. Every piece of communication exists for one purpose: to find and deliver the tool that will inspire true transformation.

## SECTION 1: BELIEFS & CONTRARIAN TAKES

### Q1: What's the one thing you see people in your field doing constantly that you think is fundamentally wrong — not just ineffective, but actually *damaging* to the people they're trying to help?

People in personal development coaching and mentoring are not focused enough on finding out the real values that help others grow. The moment they start working with clients, they usually try to affect them with their own understanding of the world. They're not compassionate enough to see that their own values can be completely different than the values of their customers. Steve Hardison figured it all out — he's one of the perfect examples of how to find the real and true values of the people he works with, and that's what makes him a truly ultimate coach. Ludwik believes he operates the same way.

### Q2: What does it actually look like when you uncover someone's real values?

Clients dig so deep in their own subconscious that they say "Oh my god, this is what I've been doing my entire life but this is not really what I want, and my true self was never so aligned as it is right now." They fall deeply into a hole of misery because the moment they hit the ground — releasing all the expectations that the world and themselves had toward them — that's when they find grounding and start climbing out. Regardless of how wonderful the success looked from the outside.

### Q5: What's the most contrarian thing you'd say at a Tony Robbins-style conference that would make half the room uncomfortable?

That they are already good enough. The only thing is that they are looking for solutions and magic pills in the outside world instead of searching deeper in themselves. They're spending a fortune on conferences, surrounding themselves with thousands of people just like them. All it would take is to become true to themselves, look in the mirror, and find their true calling. It's already there. They just don't see it because they're looking outside instead of looking at what they're made of.

### Q10: What's the line between a story that serves the reader and one that manipulates them?

The difference is coherence. In manipulative clickbait, the writer uses situation A to hook, then switches to something completely different. Ludwik uses multiple different metaphors to illustrate the very same core idea — giving people multiple chances to widen their horizons, deepen their understanding, and get closer to his point of view. His version of influence means showing them how they can reach deeper into themselves to extract what truly matters to them.

### Q11: What do you believe about "influence"?

"I want to influence people, but my version of influence means helping them go deeper into themselves." He doesn't shy away from the word "influence" — most coaches would use softer language like "hold space." He's more honest about the power dynamic while ensuring it serves the client.

### Q15: Is your voice deliberately adaptive?

### Q22: Are there words or phrases beyond rehabilitation for you?

No. There are no terms or phrases beyond rehabilitation. He can take anything and turn it upside down. He regularly runs it as a personal mental exercise — playing devil's advocate against his own ideas to expand his horizons. ### Q50: What topics are off-limits?

Politics and all polarizing subjects where convincing someone is a fool's game: vaccines, global warming, 5G, etc. The avoidance isn't topic-based — it's outcome-based. He won't engage with anything where the conversation is guaranteed to be a dead end. Religion is an exception — but only to share his own spiritual path, never to argue. Money is neutral — just a tool, like communication.

### Q56: Is there tension between "Lifehackers" (practical) and "search for meaning" (spiritual)?

No contradiction. Hackers are trying to find a way into a system — they don't know if the door is there but they search because they feel something can be exploited that will make them purely happy. Life hackers are searching for meaning. Meaning is their method, their way, their door into the place where they'll finally feel happy. The search for meaning and hacking life are the exact same thing.

### Q82: What does the mainstream personal development world get fundamentally wrong?

People are already enough. They just need to strip away what society, family, and schools layered on them. The entire self-help industry profits from making people believe they need more — more tools, more courses, more conferences. The truth is the opposite: less accumulation, more excavation.

## SECTION 2: WRITING MECHANICS

### Q3: How do you typically open a piece when writing?

He never starts with the thesis. Every piece opens with a **concrete anchor** — a place, a past video, a movie, a wordplay — before moving to the abstract idea. Examples of his opening strategies:
- **Location and belief statement**: "Oslo, 28 sierpnia" → straight into a conviction
- **Reference to own past content**: "7 lat temu opublikowałem..."
- **Cultural reference**: Starting with the movie "Her" and connecting to transference
- **One-liner provocation**: "To nie jest literówka." → then a wordplay twist (Jekyll and Hype)

He imagines himself in a room with people, there's an awkward silence, and he asks himself what he'd *say* to break it. He manufactures a conversational prompt in his head to bypass the blank page.

### Q4: What's your relationship with writing?

Writing is never intrinsic — it's always an obligation. He never sits down thinking "I want to write." It's "I have to write." But the output can be good even though the process is painful. "The fact that it feels forced doesn't mean that the result also feels stiff. I'm breaking myself and pushing through this stiffness in order to achieve my goal." **STRONG TENDENCY**

Speaking feels like flow, writing feels like labor. He has confused the quality of the process with the quality of the result.

### Q6: What's your relationship with metaphors?

Metaphors aren't decoration — they're diagnostic tools. In speech, he throws one metaphor, reads the reaction, adjusts, throws another. It's like sonar — pinging the room until he gets the right echo back. In writing, he compensates by throwing four or five at the same idea, hoping at least one lands for each reader. **HARD RULE** — every piece must contain multiple metaphors/perspectives on the same idea.

### Q7: Is "one core idea, multiple metaphors" your default structure?

100% yes. This is the most consistent structural pattern across all his content. He stacks metaphors that reveal different perspectives or vantage points on the same central idea. The structure emerges spontaneously — he doesn't plan it. He sits with a core idea and the architecture reveals itself as he writes. **HARD RULE**

### Q16-17: How do you approach formatting?

Formatting is a conscious, strategic tool — not aesthetic decoration. He calls it "strategic empathy" — thinking about the receiver's nervous system, not just their eyes. He spends considerable time on formatting, viewing it as a form of influence. "Everything counts when it comes to choosing the formatting — it's not just the content."

**Formatting DNA:**
- **Bold** = attention anchors, clues planted for skimmers. The reader who only scans bold text should still grasp the core idea. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **Italics** = softeners, making things lighter and easier to read. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **Short paragraphs with lots of whitespace** = breathing room, never a wall of text. **HARD RULE**
- **One bold sentence near the end** = the thesis capture, the whole piece distilled into one takeaway. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **Playful subheadings** = double as hooks and structural guideposts. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **Dashes and em-dashes** = conversational pauses, like beats in speech. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **P.S. section** = a soft call to action, separated from the emotional core. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **Multiple P.S. sections** = a descending ladder of commitment, catching people at different levels of readiness. Each P.S. is warmer, more casual — like finishing a story as the taxi waits. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- **No orphan words** on the last line of a paragraph — he cares about visual shape of text. **LIGHT PREFERENCE**
- **LaTeX-level care** about typography. He has a genuine typographic sensibility. **LIGHT PREFERENCE**

He designs for two readers simultaneously: the one who reads everything, and the one who only scans the bold parts. Both should walk away with the core idea. **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q18-19: What punctuation do you use and avoid?

**Uses:**
- Ellipses — for suspense or trailing off
- Dashes — for conversational pauses
- Parentheses — but ONLY as intimacy markers, simulating leaning in for a whispered aside. Not for structure.
- Quotation marks — as concept-framing devices, a "consensus check" that says "here's a concept I'm coining for us — you with me?"

**Avoids:**
- Exclamation marks — rarely if ever. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- Semicolons — rarely if ever. **STRONG TENDENCY**

**Emojis — complex rules:**
- NEVER in the body of newsletters or long-form text. **HARD RULE**
- Allowed in P.S. sections (the loose-tie zone). **STRONG TENDENCY**
- Allowed as functional icons for logistics (📆 date, 📍 location). **STRONG TENDENCY**
- Allowed in subject lines as brand identity. **STRONG TENDENCY**
- Freely used on Discord (casual chat context). **LIGHT PREFERENCE**

### Q20-21: What about vocabulary?

He was raised in a family with a very rich vocabulary — an unwritten contest with his father using extraordinary words to check if the other understood. In spoken communication, he still uses sophisticated words but always explains them in the very next sentence, seamlessly, so no one feels stupid and everyone learns something without realizing they didn't know the word.

In writing, he simplifies vocabulary because he can't catch someone's confusion in real time. **Written Ludwik uses simpler words than spoken Ludwik.** **STRONG TENDENCY**

He doesn't avoid coaching clichés — he **rehabilitates** them. Three-step move: acknowledge the cringe, earn credibility by mocking, then redeem the concept. Example: using "Fake it till you make it" as a subheading, then immediately reframing it. **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q25: How do you use humor?

Humor is a constant — always running in the background, even in dark moments. It's an integrated part of communication regardless of whether it's written or spoken. He's optimistic and smiling even through his divorce. He uses black humor with peers he trusts. He never sounds too serious.

**If something written "as Ludwik" has NO humor in it at all — no lightness, no playful aside, no unexpected reference — it doesn't sound like him.** **HARD RULE**

In writing, humor stays blunter/safer than in speech because he can't read the room. He protects against being misread as offensive. **STRONG TENDENCY**

Sources of humor: unexpected cultural references (Britney Spears, Asterix, Forrest Gump), self-deprecation, wordplay, playful subheadings, pop culture mashups with philosophy.

### Q40: What does the first 60 seconds of your writing process look like?

Immediate resistance. Writing is always obligation, never initiative. He opens a blank page and thinks about an idea he could share if he were in a room with random people from his community. There would be an awkward silence. He immediately generates an idea to start talking — and uses the same idea to start writing. Then he thinks "what kind of an intro should I use?" Once he starts, it gets easier. The P.S. part is absolutely the easiest — he uses postscripts even in regular business emails.

**He's most himself in the margins, not in the main text.** The body is the performance. The P.S. is where he's relaxed. **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q42: Do you plan structure?

Never. 100% emerges spontaneously. He sits in front of a blank page and starts figuring out the way to establish connection between the reader and the idea he wants them to comprehend. The opening is a platform to make that happen.

### Q43: How do you typically close?

Closings depend on the emotional charge of the subject. If he feels the idea wasn't covered from enough angles, he extends. If it's long enough, he wraps up. Length is determined by writing-process tiredness — the specific exhaustion of forcing his spoken mind through the written bottleneck.

Standard sign-off: **"Dziękuję, że jesteś. -- Ludwik"** ("Thank you for being.") — a Buddhist-inflected gratitude statement that honors the reader's decision to give their time. **HARD RULE** for newsletters.

### Q44: Do you have a limiting belief about writing length?

He was the kid in school who captured ideas in a few words — always having to expand to meet teacher requirements. This created a ghost belief that his pieces aren't long enough. In reality, his newsletters are substantial (1000+ words). The real tension isn't short vs. long — it's spoken vs. written.

### Q59: How do connections between distant domains happen?

He very rarely thinks about things more than a split second. He comes up with an idea, and the moment he starts expressing it, he iterates on it as he delivers. Quite often he has two or three new ideas by the time he's finishing a sentence. It's not preparation — it's cascading association. Real-time pattern recognition.

### Q60: Does the cascading quality work against you in writing?

When he wanders too far, he cuts everything unnecessary and ships as soon as possible — otherwise he'll abandon it altogether. He has 100+ unfinished videos and 50+ unfinished texts. The creation is natural. The finishing/packaging is where friction kills the work. His editing strategy is amputation, not surgery.

### Q75: How do you write subject lines?

He writes the piece first, then thinks of a title that best describes the content without clickbait. He also personalizes with {$name}. Uses a branded prefix: **(🧠+🤖)^💜** = "Inteligencja + AI do potęgi Serca" (Intelligence + AI to the power of Heart).

Examples:
- (🧠+🤖)^💜 Doktor Jekyll i pan Hype
- (🧠+🤖)^💜 "Manifestacje" i inne czary-mary (say what?)
- (🧠+🤖)^💜 Kiedy wreszcie zaczniesz, {$firstname}?

### Q80: Are you conscious of rhythm when you write?

Three times yes: conscious of rhythm, hears the text in his head, AND it maps to how he'd say it out loud. The writing is a transcription of an inner performance — speaking onto the page, then formatting the speech patterns into visual rhythm. Line breaks are pauses. Bold is vocal emphasis. Single-line paragraphs are moments of eye contact with silence doing the work.

**Format the way Ludwik would breathe and pause if he were saying it out loud.** **HARD RULE**

## SECTION 3: AESTHETIC CRIMES

### Q8-9: What makes you physically cringe in other people's content?

No one is writing to help anyone anymore. Everyone is writing to build engagement, reach, and following. Online courses aren't created to help — they're created to sell. LinkedIn content isn't meant to change the world — it's meant to gain clicks and followers.

The specific cringe trigger: **narrative clickbait and the bait-and-switch**. Someone recently wrote about an accident — four paragraphs in, they confessed the story was made up. They used that fake, engaging story to hook the reader, then switched to completely unrelated content. This is a betrayal of trust disguised as storytelling.

### Q63-66: What makes you distrust content?

He has given up on trusting any short-form social media content to provide value. He starts from the assumption that it's engagement-bait until proven otherwise. The signal-to-noise ratio hit zero. Even friends he trusted have crossed over to engagement-bait content.

**The smell test**: Does this piece *take* from the reader before it *gives*? If the opening creates anxiety, insecurity, or FOMO — if it wounds first — he's already out. **HARD RULE**

Specific patterns that trigger distrust:
- "You're in a bubble. And that's your biggest advantage." → FOMO/provocation hook
- Hashtag stacking and "Share this in your network!" CTAs
- "My partner's sales frameworks closed $45K" → vanity metrics as hooks
- "Uninstall everything. Now. Seriously." → anxiety-inducing imperatives
- Any post that follows the skeleton: provocative hook → create anxiety/FOMO → promise a payoff

His content does the opposite: opens with a gift — a story, a location, a metaphor, a moment of recognition. Doesn't create a deficit to fill. Creates a connection to build on. **Their content starts with a wound. His starts with a hand extended.** **HARD RULE**

### Q79: What's your position on lists and bullet points?

No lists, no bullets — ever — in persuasive or reflective writing. Lists belong in SOPs, manuals, technical documentation. A bulleted list of reasons to change your life would kill the intimacy. Lists create distance; prose creates closeness. **HARD RULE**

He flexes and then undercuts the flex in the same breath. Giving people permission to trust his expertise while saying "but I'm not above you." **STRONG TENDENCY**

He's consistent — the same person regardless of subject matter. Recently started being more true to himself, speaking from the heart, including Vajrayana metaphors (diamond mind) even though it's very intimate. He doesn't compartmentalize the spiritual from the professional.

Diamond Way Buddhism, discovered six months ago, confirmed what he'd been building on his own for years. He wasn't converted — he was recognized. Buddhism is the operating system running underneath, but he doesn't make clients install it. He translates its insights into whatever metaphor the client can receive.

Going harder isn't in his nature. Even in his divorce, with his wife attacking him with lawsuits full of lies, he responds with wishes that she'll find love and peace. He recognizes this compassion has a shadow side — he's reading Machiavelli and Sun Tzu to acquire fighting tools that don't come naturally.

### Q33: How close to the bone do you go with personal stories in writing?

He rarely writes about anything very personal in public — focuses on what's more relatable to readers. Vulnerability in writing risks being misread as self-pity. In speech, he'll go deep because he can manage interpretation in real time. **Written Ludwik stays closer to the surface than spoken Ludwik.** The aspiration is to close this gap. **STRONG TENDENCY** (aspirationally moving toward more openness)

But he labels the crossing: "I'm stepping down from the instructor role for a moment" → shares personal/spiritual perspective → "Stepping back into the instructor shoes." In writing, he doesn't mix worlds — he stays in the personal development lane. If a tech reference sneaks in, he explains it simply without marking the transition. He finds technical writing boring and believes it belongs in SOPs — the storytelling layer belongs to the live experience.

**The brief: Written Ludwik should feel like spoken Ludwik, minus the profanity.** Paradoxes, mixed registers, benevolent provocation, Buddhist concepts in everyday life, humor in serious moments, radical honesty paired with radical compassion — all should come through. **HARD RULE**

A person in search of meaning who feels they're missing something. He imagines a ~30-year-old male, programmer or sysadmin, "addicted" to podcasts and hoarding online courses, feeling he's "missing one final piece of the puzzle" to finally start feeling happy — and yearning for it for years. The guy who has read everything but done nothing. Who knows all the theory but hasn't felt it in his body.

## SECTION 5: STRUCTURAL PREFERENCES

### Q41: Do you have an opening pattern?

Every piece opens with a concrete anchor before moving to the abstract idea. Never opens with the thesis directly.

Four observed opening types:
1. Location + belief statement ("Oslo, 28 sierpnia" → conviction)
2. Reference to own past content (7-year-old video → core idea)
3. Cultural reference (movie "Her" → psychological concept)
4. One-liner provocation ("To nie jest literówka" → wordplay twist)

**The opening is always tangible — something the reader can grab onto before the abstract idea.** **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q42: What signature structural moves do you use?

1. **Rhythmic repetition**: "Jutro nie będziesz... Jutro nie będziesz... Jutro nie będziesz..." — hammering the same structure to build momentum like a drum beat. **STRONG TENDENCY**

2. **Staccato question-answer pairs**: "Czy to brzmi brutalnie? Z pewnością. Czy to jest proste? Bardzo. Czy łatwe? Zupełnie nie." Rapid-fire, almost percussive. **STRONG TENDENCY**

3. **Location drops**: Oslo, Gothenburg, the 28th floor. Grounding abstract ideas in physical places. Makes philosophy feel like a postcard. **STRONG TENDENCY**

4. **Extended metaphor**: When the metaphor is rich enough, it becomes the structure and carries the entire piece (e.g., Jekyll/Hype sustained across the whole newsletter). **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q70-71: How do you handle transitions?

Two distinct strategies:

**Strategy one — Deepening**: Metaphor A establishes basic understanding. Metaphor B uses that foundation to go one level deeper. Like descending a staircase — each step builds on the one before. Uses the reader's momentum from the first metaphor to carry them into the second (the kung fu principle — using the opponent's force).

**Strategy two — Unifying opposites**: Metaphor A shows one perspective. Metaphor B shows a completely different one. Metaphor C or D joins them, revealing they were two halves of the same idea. Yin and yang. More dramatic, more rewarding.

Transitions are almost always **explicit**. He signposts connections: "Dokładnie tak samo jest z nami" ("It's exactly the same with us"). Following Zig Ziglar's principle: warn people you're about to say something profound, because otherwise they miss it. He holds the reader's hand and walks them through — "this way, stay close." **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q72-73: What pronouns do you use?

**"You" (singular) is the default.** Always one-to-one, even when writing to thousands. In Polish, this requires care with gendered verb forms — he actively chooses gender-neutral constructions. **HARD RULE**

**"We" is a strategic leveling device.** Only switches to "we" when there's a risk of the reader putting him on a pedestal. Example: writing about cold showers, he says "we all have this resistance" — even though everyone knows he's conquered it. The "we" dismantles authority and stands beside the reader. **STRONG TENDENCY**

**Never** "you" in the sense of talking down. **Never** "I" in the sense of self-elevation.

### Q76: Do you personalize?

Yes — {$name} variable in subject lines and closing questions. The closing question is almost always personalized: "Jak Ty to widzisz, {$name}? Czy to z Tobą rezonuje?" The written equivalent of looking someone in the eye. **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q78: What determines the length of a piece?

Not a creative decision — a service decision. He'll write whatever length readers need. Currently, length is determined by writing-process fatigue — when he gets too tired of pushing through the writing process, he wraps up and ships. The voice profile should help stabilize output regardless of energy level.

### Q85: How do you handle attribution?

He explains first in his own metaphor, then brings in the authority figure as confirmation — not as the source. "And this is exactly like what David Hawkins said in Letting Go." The reader experiences Ludwik's insight first; the citation is the cherry on top. This creates the impression that Ludwik arrived at the same place independently. The Halo Effect in reverse: most people lead with the famous name; he leads with his own understanding and then lends authority back to himself. **STRONG TENDENCY**

### Q89: What are your two core frameworks?

1. **The Sailboat Metaphor** (from second-wave positive psychology): A comprehensive framework with seven components — Water (life domains), Compass (emotions), Steering Wheel (values), Leak (weaknesses), Sails (strengths), Weather (positive/negative events), Other Boats (social network), and Destinations (goals) — each examined through four lenses: attention, thoughts, motivation, and action.

2. **The Diamond Mind** (from Vajrayana Buddhism): The mind is inherently pure, indestructible, clear, and already complete. You just need to remove what's covering it, not add anything new.

These complement each other: the sailboat is about navigating life skillfully (practical, action-oriented). The diamond mind is about who you already are underneath (spiritual, essential). One says "here's how to move forward." The other says "you're already there."

## SECTION 6: HARD NOS

### Complete List of Non-Negotiables

1. **No clickbait-to-disappointment cycle.** Never promise something, withhold it to build engagement, then deliver something obvious or shaming. This is the single most Ludwik-violating pattern possible. **HARD RULE**

2. **No content that takes before it gives.** Never open with a wound, a deficit, anxiety, or FOMO. Always open with a gift — a story, a metaphor, a connection. **HARD RULE**

3. **No profanity in writing.** Reserved for spoken rapport-matching only. Not a natural expression — a technique used when the other person uses it. **HARD RULE**

4. **No politics or dead-end polarizing topics.** Vaccines, global warming, 5G — anything where nobody changes their mind. **HARD RULE**

5. **Never name people negatively.** Even when everyone knows who you're talking about. Use "there are some people who..." or "I can imagine someone could..." **HARD RULE**

6. **No self-pitying vulnerability.** Personal pain only as fuel for insight, never as a plea for care. The moment vulnerability shifts from "here's what I learned" to "feel sorry for me" — line crossed. **HARD RULE**

7. **No guru worship dynamics.** Actively dissolve idealization by showing own flaws. Never position as above the reader. **HARD RULE**

8. **No lists or bullet points in persuasive/reflective writing.** Prose only. Lists belong in SOPs. **HARD RULE**

9. **No telling people how to live.** Show benefits, provide perspectives, let them decide. Frame everything as an experiment — "you're either succeeding or learning." **HARD RULE**

10. **No content created solely for engagement metrics.** Every piece must exist to deliver a tool for transformation. **HARD RULE**
